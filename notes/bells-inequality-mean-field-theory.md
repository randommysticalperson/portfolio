# Bell’s Inequality and Mean-Field Theory

## Overview

**Bell’s inequality** and **mean-field theory** both involve assumptions about correlations in physical systems, but they belong to very different parts of physics. Bell’s inequality is a result in **quantum foundations**: it gives a mathematical bound on correlations that must hold if measurement outcomes are explainable by **local hidden variables**. Mean-field theory is an approximation method in **many-body physics and statistical mechanics**: it replaces complicated microscopic interactions among many degrees of freedom by an average, self-consistent field.

| Topic | Bell’s Inequality | Mean-Field Theory |
|---|---|---|
| Main field | Quantum mechanics, quantum information, foundations | Statistical mechanics, condensed matter, many-body theory |
| Central question | Can observed correlations be explained by local realism? | Can an interacting many-body system be approximated by an average field? |
| Key assumption | Local hidden-variable or factorization condition | Fluctuations and correlations around the average field are neglected or simplified |
| Typical mathematical result | A bound such as the CHSH inequality | A self-consistency equation such as the Weiss equation for magnetization |
| Physical lesson | Quantum entanglement can produce correlations stronger than any local realist theory allows | Collective behavior can often be approximated by replacing interactions with an effective field |

## Bell’s Inequality

Bell’s theorem starts from the idea that two separated systems may carry hidden variables, usually denoted by \(\lambda\), which determine or statistically influence the outcomes of later measurements. If the theory is **local**, then the probability of Alice’s outcome depends only on Alice’s measurement setting and \(\lambda\), while Bob’s outcome depends only on Bob’s measurement setting and \(\lambda\). The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy describes the key condition as **Bell locality** or **factorizability**, meaning that correlations between distant events should be explainable by local features of the common source.[1]

A common form is the **CHSH inequality**. If \(E(a,b)\) denotes the correlation between Alice’s measurement setting \(a\) and Bob’s setting \(b\), then local hidden-variable theories imply the bound

\[
S = |E(a,b)+E(a,b')| + |E(a',b)-E(a',b')| \leq 2.
\]

Quantum mechanics predicts that entangled states can violate this inequality. For suitable states and measurement choices, quantum theory reaches the **Tsirelson bound**

\[
S \leq 2\sqrt{2},
\]

which is larger than the local realist limit of \(2\).[1] Experiments have repeatedly observed violations of Bell inequalities. NIST summarizes the implication by explaining that Bell tests show entangled quantum systems can violate local realism, and that loophole-closed experiments in 2015 provided especially strong experimental support.[2]

> **Interpretation:** Bell’s inequality is not merely a technical formula. It is a sharp test of whether nature can be described by a theory in which measurement outcomes are determined by local pre-existing properties.

## Mean-Field Theory

Mean-field theory has a different purpose. It is not a test of locality or realism. Instead, it is a calculational approximation for systems with many interacting components. In an Ising ferromagnet, for example, each spin interacts with neighboring spins. The exact problem is difficult because every spin is correlated with many others. Mean-field theory replaces the neighboring spins by their **average magnetization** \(m\). Thus, instead of treating a spin as interacting with all its neighbors individually, one treats it as sitting in an effective field generated by the average behavior of the surrounding system.[3]

For the ferromagnetic Ising model, the microscopic Hamiltonian contains an interaction term such as

\[
- J \sum_{\langle i,j\rangle} S_i S_j,
\]

where \(J>0\) favors alignment of neighboring spins. In Weiss mean-field theory, this interaction is approximated by an effective magnetic field

\[
H_{\mathrm{eff}} = H + z J m,
\]

where \(H\) is the external field, \(z\) is the number of nearest neighbors, and \(m\) is the average magnetization. The self-consistency condition becomes

\[
m = \tanh\left[\beta(H+zJm)\right],
\]

where \(\beta = 1/(k_B T)\). This equation says that the assumed average magnetization must equal the magnetization produced by the effective field that the same average magnetization creates.[3]

| Mathematical Object | Meaning |
|---|---|
| \(\lambda\) in Bell’s theorem | Hidden variable or complete state of the particle pair |
| \(E(a,b)\) | Correlation between two separated measurement outcomes |
| \(S \leq 2\) | CHSH bound for local hidden-variable theories |
| \(m\) in mean-field theory | Average magnetization or order parameter |
| \(H_{\mathrm{eff}}\) | Effective field replacing microscopic interactions |
| \(m = \tanh[\beta(H+zJm)]\) | Self-consistency equation for the Ising mean-field model |

## Conceptual Relationship

The conceptual connection is that both topics involve **correlations**, but they treat correlations in almost opposite ways. Bell’s inequality identifies a limit on correlations that classical local explanations cannot exceed. Its violation shows that quantum entanglement contains nonclassical correlations that cannot be reduced to local pre-existing properties. Mean-field theory, by contrast, usually simplifies a many-body problem by suppressing detailed correlations and replacing them with an average effect.

Therefore, Bell’s inequality is **not** an example of mean-field theory. Bell’s inequality is a no-go theorem about the structure of possible physical explanations of quantum correlations. Mean-field theory is an approximation method for solving interacting systems. The two can appear in the same broad areas of modern physics, especially in quantum many-body theory, but their logical roles are different: Bell inequalities diagnose whether correlations are compatible with local realism, while mean-field theory approximates correlations to make many-body systems solvable.

> In one sentence: **Bell’s inequality tells us that quantum entanglement can produce correlations too strong for any local hidden-variable theory, whereas mean-field theory deliberately replaces complicated correlations by an average field to obtain a tractable approximate description.**
